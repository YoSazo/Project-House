The Survey Robot is a compact autonomous rover with a self-leveling, 4-point stabilized, telescoping probe system that classifies buildability by combining local bracing signals, penetration data, neighborhood inference, and confidence-based adaptive surveying.

1. Purpose
The Survey Robot solves the site qualification problem: determining, autonomously, which areas of raw undeveloped ground are safe to build on — without requiring a human geotechnical engineer at every site and without turning the robot into an overbuilt, expensive machine.

It does this by making the probing zone — not the whole robot — the intelligent part. Local ground-coupling replaces chassis mass. Swarm inference replaces individual sensor certainty. Adaptive convergence replaces brute-force uniform sampling.

Its output is a site heatmap: a confidence-scored spatial classification of every zone as BUILDABLE, MARGINAL, or REJECT. This heatmap is the gate that unlocks the rest of the House Brain build sequence.

3. C2A Transmutation Chain
Nine constraints were identified and transmuted from first principles. Each transmutation was derived by analogy before any engineering solution was named.

Constraint 1 — The Push-Up Problem
Pushing a probe into compacted dry ground requires serious downward force. But the robot base is light. Instead of the probe going down, the rover slips, lifts, or tips — producing noisy resistance data.

Stop asking the whole robot body to resist probe force.
Place the probe centerline directly under the robot's center of mass — keeps force symmetric.
Add an outer sleeve / stabilizing collar that contacts the ground first.
The inner probe rod then travels through that stabilized guide.
Load path becomes: ground ↔ outer brace ↔ inner probe — not ground ↔ probe ↔ robot chassis.
Result: light rover + local ground-coupled probe fixture, not a heavy expensive rig.

Constraint 2 — The Crooked Straw Problem
The outer sleeve can stabilize the inner probe, but only if it contacts the ground cleanly. On uneven terrain or debris-covered surfaces, the sleeve may seat at an angle, rock during probing, or partially grip — producing bad data even though the bracing concept is correct.

Don't force the ground to be flat. Make the probe system adapt to the local angle.
Mount the outer sleeve on a self-leveling / compliant joint — not rigidly fixed to the chassis.

Constraint 3 — The Fake Firmness Problem
Even with a self-leveling sleeve, contact can be false: one edge on a rock, gravel under one side, or soft crust over weaker soil. The sleeve may appear seated, but under real load it settles suddenly, slips sideways, or rotates — corrupting the force-depth curve.

Don't let the sleeve rely on one contact ring alone.
Use a 4-point support frame around the center probe. The four feet touch down first.
Multiple contact points detect uneven support, redistribute load, and create a wider, truer base.
The 4-point frame converts unstable contact into distributed support.
Intelligence stays local to the probing zone — the whole rover does not need to become heavy.

Constraint 4 — The Wobbly Table Problem
A 4-point frame looks stable, but if one leg is barely touching and another is taking most of the weight, it can still wobble under probe load. The robot needs to know whether each foot is actually carrying load before committing to a full probe.

Don't just measure whether the 4 feet happen to share load. Make the robot force them to share load equally.
Each outer support leg has a small actuator or compliant preload mechanism.
A simple per-foot force sensor or motor-current proxy measures contact load.
A control rule equalizes contact force across all 4 points before probing begins.
The probe platform doesn't passively rest on the ground — it actively clamps into a stable stance.

Constraint 5 — The Cardboard Floor Problem
Equal grip across 4 feet does not guarantee the shallow layer is strong enough to act as a brace. The robot can equalize perfectly on soft crust, loose sand, or brittle top layer — and the whole platform still sinks or drifts once the center probe adds deeper force.

Separate the job of holding force from the job of sensing depth.
Outer sleeve / anchor tube goes in first and becomes the shallow structural brace.
Inner probe rod travels through the stabilized sleeve and does the actual sensing.

Constraint 6 — The Tent Peg Problem
A tent peg works great in firm ground. In loose sand or shallow fill, the peg enters but doesn't hold. The outer sleeve can enter weak near-surface soil but still provides no trustworthy reaction base for the deeper inner probe.

If the shallow layer cannot support the outer brace, that itself is valuable survey data — not just a failure.
Shallow brace failure indicates: loose sand, weak fill, fractured crust, or poor near-surface support.
The robot does not say 'I failed.' It says: 'This location failed shallow bracing — that is a classification event.'
Logged as: brace_mode_failed / surface_hold_low / shallow_support_unreliable.
This feeds directly into site classification: REJECT, MARGINAL, or flag for alternate probe mode.
Result: weak shallow ground becomes an early hazard indicator that saves time, energy, and mechanism wear.

Constraint 7 — The False Shortcut Problem
Shallow brace failure is valuable data — but the robot must know whether it means 'this point is genuinely bad ground,' or 'this is just a weak surface layer over something worth probing,' or 'relocate a meter and retry.' Treating all brace failures identically produces wrong classifications.

Don't force certainty from a single failed probe point.

Constraint 8 — The Pixel Problem
Once the robot starts inferring from neighboring points, it needs rules for: how many nearby points are enough, how far 'nearby' means, when to trust interpolation, when to densify the survey grid, and when to stop. Without this the survey either under-samples or over-samples.

The robot should not ask: 'Did this one point solve the ground?'
It should ask: 'What ground-model am I converging toward as more points come in?'

Constraint 9 — The Poke-the-Mattress Problem
The robot now converges toward a site model as it probes. But it needs a stop rule: when is a zone confident enough? When do we stop probing a good area? When do we accept 'good enough' and move on? Without this the survey chases certainty forever and becomes too slow.

Poke broad first — sparse initial grid across the full site area.
Find suspicious transitions — where zone classification changes between adjacent points.

5.2 Probe Assembly — Outer Sleeve
The outer sleeve is the bracing component. It is not the sensor. Its only job is to give the inner probe a stable, ground-anchored guide.

Diameter: 40 – 60 mm, hardened steel or carbide-tipped leading edge
Shallow penetration target: 200 – 400 mm — enough for reliable frictional hold in most soils
Self-leveling mount: passive gimbal or low-stiffness compliant joint, corrects up to ±12° from chassis tilt
4-point outer foot frame: four feet deploy radially around the sleeve base at 90° intervals
Per-foot actuators: small linear actuators equalize contact force across all 4 feet before probing
Per-foot load sensing: motor-current proxy or inline load cell — confirms equal grip
Brace failure flag: if equalization cannot be achieved within threshold, log brace_mode_failed and proceed to neighborhood inference

5.3 Probe Assembly — Inner Rod
The inner rod is the sensing component. It travels through the stabilized outer sleeve and records the force-depth relationship that classifies the soil.

Diameter: 20 – 35 mm — fits through outer sleeve with clearance
Max probe depth: 2.0 – 3.0 m depending on soil and build requirements
Penetration force: up to 200 N — enough for most non-rocky soils
Load cell: inline, measures continuous penetration force at 10 Hz minimum
Depth encoder: tracks inner rod position to ±5 mm
Retraction: motorized, with stall detection if rod gets stuck
Tip: interchangeable — standard cone for normal soils, narrow spike for rocky/dry conditions

5.4 Moisture Conditioning Ring
Optional sub-system. Small reservoir (200 – 500 mL) with gravity-fed ring dispenser around the outer sleeve base. Only activated when the probe classification model determines granular dry soil and current moisture is below threshold. Never used on fine-grained soils.

7.2 Neighborhood Inference Rules
1 bad point surrounded by 4+ BUILDABLE points → reclassify as LOCAL ANOMALY / MARGINAL
3+ adjacent REJECT points → confirm as WEAK ZONE / REJECT; stop sampling that zone
Mixed BUILDABLE / REJECT boundary → trigger densified sampling (halve grid spacing) along boundary
UNKNOWN point → probe 2 adjacent points; use their curves to infer probable classification
Inference search radius: 1.5× grid spacing for initial survey, 0.75× grid spacing for densified zones

7.3 Adaptive Survey Protocol
Phase 1 — Sparse grid: probe at standard spacing across full site polygon
Phase 2 — Boundary densification: halve grid spacing at every zone boundary with confidence < 0.75
Phase 3 — Anomaly resolution: probe 4 adjacent points around every UNKNOWN or isolated REJECT
Stop rule: survey ends when no zone boundary has moved meaningfully in the last 3 new probe points along that boundary, AND overall uncertainty score is below configured threshold
Override: House Brain can set a maximum probe count budget; robot optimizes point placement within budget

Motor-current proxy for load sensing — avoids dedicated force-torque sensors on each foot
No humanoid complexity — no arms, no legs, no balance systems
Local stabilization frame — makes the probing zone smart, not the whole machine heavy
Adaptive survey — stops early when certainty is sufficient; does not over-probe stable zones
Shared reference patch function — Survey Robot produces the Logistics Robot's calibration stamp at zero extra cost

14. Success Criteria
The Survey Robot is successful when it can:

Complete a full site survey autonomously without human intervention
Produce repeatable point classifications on the same soil type across different robots
Distinguish clearly weak zones from usable zones in a single survey pass
Convert probe failure into informative data rather than dead stops
Emit a site heatmap that the House Brain can use to place the build zone without additional human survey input
Avoid excessive oversampling by stopping when new probes stop changing the decision
Produce the grade reference patch for the Logistics Robot as part of the normal survey completion sequence
Operate correctly when individual sensors degrade — failing gracefully rather than catastrophically
