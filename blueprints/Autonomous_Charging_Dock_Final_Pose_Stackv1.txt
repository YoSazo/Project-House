0. Purpose

This blueprint captures the full constraint-transmutation sequence used
to solve the following engineering wall:

How do you make robot charging autonomous when physical connector mating
is a hard robotics problem, without breaking the autonomy story by
requiring manual cable plugging?

This is not solved by making the robot better at plugging things in. It
is solved by trading the precision burden away.

1. Original Wall

The charging protocol is underspecified for autonomy. The blueprint says
"robot docks charging port to post cable" but that compresses a huge
robotics problem into one word: docks.

Autonomous charging is not mainly about voltage, current, or software
handshake. It is mainly about:

  - alignment
  - connector mating
  - tolerance
  - dirt
  - repeated cycles
  - approach error
  - recovery from slight imperfection

If a human must physically plug the cable in, the autonomy story breaks.

The real wall is:

  How do you make autonomous charging work without requiring the robot
  to perform delicate plug-in precision every single time?

2. Transmutation Chain

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #1 — Turn Plugging Into Walking In

  Constraint: The Blind Person Threading a Needle
  Autonomous connector mating is like threading a needle blindfolded
  on an uneven floor. The problem is precision physical mating in the
  real world, not electricity.

  Trade: Make the charger face outward. As the robot moves forward it
  comes into contact. The more it moves forward the more it locks in.
  At a point it is fully locked in and now charging. Simply walk in.

  Old burden: delicate connector mating every cycle
  New burden: dock geometry must accept imperfect approach

  Progress: 16%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #2 — Make Wrong Entry Spend Itself Into Right Entry

  Constraint: The Shopping Cart Into the Checkout Cradle
  Walk-in entry helps, but what if the robot comes in crooked, one
  wheel on dirt, diagonal, or jams halfway?

  Trade: Self-correcting capture geometry. Rounded corners and funnel
  geometry use the robot's momentum and forward motion as correction.
  Whatever the entry angle, the dock actively guides it to the same
  final spot. The dock manufactures correctness from imperfect approach.

  Old burden: imperfect entry risks jamming or half-seating
  New burden: correction must happen gently enough for repeated cycles

  Progress: 28%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #3 — Spend Time to Save Hardware

  Constraint: The Bowling Ball Into the Gutter Funnel
  Self-correcting dock can still fail through impact energy, abrasion,
  repeated correction loads, bounce-back, and guide-surface wear.

  Trade: When battery falls below threshold, robot enters low-battery
  charge-seeking mode — reduced speed, reduced acceleration, gentle
  final approach. The dock no longer has to correct a fast heavy body.
  It only has to correct a gentle one.

  Old burden: high-energy entry causes slamming and wear
  New burden: slow approach still has to guarantee true final seating

  Progress: 40%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #4 — Let Touch Wake the Final Capture

  Constraint: The Sleepy Person Still Missing the Bed
  Slow approach prevents violence. It does not guarantee full final
  seating — still possible to stop too early or end in half-dock.

  Trade: First valid contact pressure allows the dock to finish the
  last bit of capture. Robot only has to get close enough. The dock
  handles the last precision.

  Old burden: slow approach may still end in gentle half-dock
  New burden: must prove first contact became full final charge state

  Progress: 52%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #5 — Add Intent / Session Communication

  Constraint: The Handshake Is Not the Conversation
  Touch alone is too ambiguous. Is this a charging event or random
  bumping?

  Trade: Robot and dock communicate session intent. Dock knows robot
  identity, battery state, that this approach is intentional. Touch
  plus session handshake reduces ambiguity.

  Old burden: touch alone too ambiguous
  New burden: handshake and intent still need physical proof of charging

  Progress: 64%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #6 — Charging Physically Impossible Until Final Pose

  Constraint: The Card Insert Is Not the Payment
  Handshake and intent prove context. They do not prove actual power
  transfer or successful seating.

  Trade: Design the dock so that electrical contact is physically
  impossible during correction phase. Guide surfaces do the correction.
  Charging contacts are recessed/isolated during approach. Only the one
  true final docked pose exposes the charging path. If charging = true,
  then full seating = true. Current flow becomes physical proof.

  Old burden: handshake does not prove final seated charging
  New burden: keep final-pose-only contact system reliable over life

  Progress: 88%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Transmutation #7 — TTL / Treat Dock Surfaces as Service Parts

  Constraint: The Light Switch That Only Works When Door Fully Closed
  Final-pose-only charging is excellent, but guide rails, capture
  faces, and charging contacts wear from dirt, repeated cycles, and
  outdoor exposure.

  Trade: Stop trying to make guide/contact surfaces eternal. Treat
  them as TTL-rated service parts — inspectable, cleanable,
  replaceable. The hard autonomy problem becomes a routine maintenance
  problem.

  Old burden: keep final-pose contacts perfect forever
  New burden: inspection cadence, replacement intervals, spare inventory

  Progress: 100%

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

3. Final Solved Form

The original wall:

  The charging protocol is underspecified for autonomy. "Robot docks"
  hand-waves over a hard robotics problem.

was traded into:

  A low-speed, self-correcting, walk-in charging dock whose geometry
  converts imperfect approach into one final seating pose, where
  electrical contact is physically impossible before full convergence,
  and whose guide/contact surfaces are TTL maintenance components.

4. Dock Architecture

  A. Charging doctrine

  Do not design charging as delicate plug insertion or fine connector
  mating. Design charging as: coarse approach, guided capture,
  geometric correction, final-pose-only contact.

  B. Robot docking doctrine

  When battery falls below threshold, robot enters charge-seeking mode:
  - reduced speed
  - reduced acceleration
  - gentle final approach
  - session intent broadcast to target dock

  C. Dock physical doctrine

  The dock must:
  - present outward-facing capture geometry
  - accept imperfect approach within a correction envelope
  - use rounded/channeled guide surfaces to self-center
  - convert diagonal entry into straight final alignment
  - separate correction surfaces from final charging contact surfaces
  - make electrical contact physically impossible during approach/
    correction phase

  D. Final truth doctrine

  Charging is not possible during: crooked entry, half seating,
  correction phase, almost-there states. Charging is only possible
  when the robot is in the one true final docked pose.

  If charging = true, then full seating = true.

  E. Session doctrine

  Robot and dock communicate before and during charging:
  - Robot broadcasts: chassis_id, battery_soc_pct, charge request
  - Dock confirms: post_id, house_id, available_power_w
  - Charging begins only after: physical final pose + session handshake
  - House Brain tracks all charging sessions

  F. Failure doctrine

  The dock fails into NO CHARGE rather than FALSE CHARGE. Degraded
  alignment or contact should more readily cause refusal to confirm
  charge than accidental unsafe charging state. Truth stays
  conservative.

  G. Lifecycle doctrine

  Serviceable dock components:
  - guide rails / funnel surfaces (TTL by cycle count)
  - final charging contact pads (TTL by cycle count + resistance check)
  - centering channel surfaces (TTL by wear inspection)
  - all front-accessible, modular, removable without disassembling
    the whole post, standardized across all dock stations

5. What This Obsoletes in the Parasitic Power Network

The previous parasitic_power_network.txt described charging as:

  - "Connector: standardized industrial DC connector, keyed, IP65"
  - "Cable reach: 1.5m retractable coiled cable"
  - "Physical connection — robot docks charging port to post cable"

Those descriptions assumed a cable-based plug-in connection.

The walk-in dock architecture replaces this with:

  - No cable. No plug. No retractable cord.
  - Dock is a fixed geometry mounted on the post.
  - Robot drives into the dock capture zone.
  - Final electrical contact is integrated into the dock body,
    not carried on a cable.
  - All electrical specs unchanged: 48V DC, 30A, ~1.4kW.
  - All session/routing logic unchanged.
  - Physical interface fundamentally different.

6. Remaining Living Constraint

The autonomy gap is closed. What remains is operational:

  - dock guide/contact surface TTL intervals
  - inspection cadence per dock station
  - cleaning procedure for guide/capture area
  - replacement part inventory at Factory-in-a-Box
  - threshold-based service when correction quality degrades
  - service logs tied to docking cycle counts
