  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Core Doctrine: The robots never commute to charge. The houses they
  build become the charging infrastructure. The power grid expands
  organically with the city. Every completed house is a new energy node.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  One-Sentence Summary: Every completed house includes a standardized
  exterior charging post powered by its solar array, creating a
  distributed charging network that grows with construction — eliminating
  the need for central charging stations and letting robots charge within
  50 meters of any active build site.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Purpose

If 100 robot clusters are running and each must drive 2 miles to a
central solar farm to charge, they waste 30% of their day commuting
for energy. The doubling rate dies not from mechanical failure but from
energy logistics.

The Parasitic Power Network solves this by making every house a
charging node. The swarm builds its own power grid as it builds the
city.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The first house powers the robots building the second house. The
  second house powers robots building the third and fourth. The network
  grows exponentially because the infrastructure IS the output.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

2. Architecture

2.1 House Solar Array

Every house design includes rooftop solar panels as a base feature.

  - Array size: scaled to house size, minimum 3kW per house
  - Inverter/charge controller integrated into house electrical system
  - House battery bank (LiFePO4, matching Skateboard chemistry) for
    nighttime use and buffering
  - Excess daytime generation is available for robot charging

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  During construction hours when the house is unoccupied, the solar
  panels generate power with no residential load. All excess goes to
  the charging post.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

2.2 Charging Post — Walk-In Dock Architecture

Every house exterior includes one standardized charging dock. Built into
the house as a base feature during construction — not added after.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The charging dock is NOT a cable or plug. It is a fixed walk-in
  capture geometry that the robot drives into. See: Autonomous Charging
  Dock + Final-Pose Truth Stack blueprint for the full transmutation
  chain behind this architecture.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  Physical Specification:
  - Mounting: bolted to exterior wall or standalone post within 2m of
    house
  - Height: 600mm from ground level (matches Skateboard charging port
    height)
  - Dock type: walk-in self-correcting capture geometry — no cable, no
    retractable cord, no plug
  - Capture envelope: accepts robot approach within ±15° yaw and
    ±50mm lateral offset, self-corrects to final pose
  - Guide surfaces: rounded/channeled funnel geometry that converts
    diagonal or off-center entry into straight final alignment
  - Final charging contact: recessed DC contact pads, exposed only
    at true final docked position — physically impossible to energize
    during approach or correction phase
  - Voltage: 48V DC (matches Skateboard battery system)
  - Current: 30A continuous maximum
  - Charging power: ~1.4kW per post
  - Status LED: Green (available), Blue (charging), Amber (limited
    power), Red (fault), Off (no power)

  Protection:
  - Over-current protection at the post
  - Ground fault detection
  - Reverse polarity protection (contact geometry prevents it)
  - Weather sealed — rated for outdoor exposure in all deployment
    climates
  - Guide surfaces and contact pads are IP65 when no robot docked
  - Failure mode: fails into NO-CHARGE rather than FALSE-CHARGE —
    degraded contacts refuse to confirm charge rather than risk
    unsafe charging state

  Serviceable Components (TTL-rated):
  - Guide rails / funnel capture surfaces — replaceable inserts,
    TTL by cycle count
  - Final charging contact pads — replaceable cartridge, TTL by
    cycle count + contact resistance check
  - Centering channel surfaces — inspectable, replaceable on wear
  - All front-accessible, modular, removable without disassembling
    the whole post, standardized across all dock stations
  - Dock service parts stocked in Factory-in-a-Box Zone B inventory

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Same dock. Same voltage. Same protocol. Every house. Every city.
  Every dock is interchangeable. A robot built in Batch 1 charges at a
  dock built on House 10,000.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

2.3 Charging Protocol

  1. House Brain routes robot to nearest available dock with sufficient
     power (robot does not choose — House Brain schedules)
  2. Robot enters charge-seeking mode when battery below threshold:
     reduced speed, reduced acceleration, gentle final approach
  3. Robot broadcasts session intent to target dock: chassis_id,
     battery_soc_pct, requested_charge_w
  4. Robot approaches dock capture zone (guided by UWB positioning)
  5. Robot enters self-correcting funnel geometry — dock guide surfaces
     correct imperfect approach angle into final alignment
  6. Robot reaches final docked pose — electrical contact pads engage
     (contact is physically impossible before full seating)
  7. Dock confirms charging session:
     - Dock reports: available_power_w, post_id, house_id
     - Robot confirms: chassis_id, battery_soc_pct, charging = true
     - charging = true IS the physical proof of final seating
  8. Charging begins at negotiated rate
  9. House Brain tracks: which robot, which dock, charge rate, duration
  10. Charge complete or House Brain reassigns robot → robot backs out
      of dock under low-speed reverse
  11. Dock LED returns to Green (available)

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Charging decision is House Brain's job, not the robot's. House Brain
  knows which robots need charge, which docks are available, and which
  robots can afford to pause. Optimal scheduling, not first-come.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

3. Network Topology

3.1 Day Zero (Before Any Houses Exist)

  - Factory-in-a-Box container has its own solar array (roof-mounted)
  - Container provides initial charging posts (2–4) for seed cluster
  - Seed cluster operates within range of container charging
  - Battery swap also available from container inventory as backup

3.2 After House #1 Complete

  - House #1 solar array goes live
  - House #1 charging post becomes active node
  - Robots building House #2 can charge at House #1 (typically 20–50m
    away)
  - Container still available as backup

3.3 After N Houses Complete

  - N charging posts active across the emerging neighborhood
  - Robot assignment considers proximity to nearest available post
  - As construction front moves away from early houses, power network
    follows because each new house adds a new post at the frontier

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The charging frontier always matches the construction frontier. There
  is never a gap between where robots work and where robots charge.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

3.4 Steady State

  - Dense neighborhood: charging posts every 10–30m
  - Robots charge opportunistically between tasks
  - House Brain routes to nearest available post with sufficient power
  - Nighttime: house batteries buffer solar, posts still available
    (lower rate)
  - Grid resilience: no single point of failure — dust storm takes out
    20% of panels, swarm routes to remaining posts automatically

4. Battery Sizing Implication

The Parasitic Power Network changes the Skateboard battery design
calculus.

  Without parasitic charging:
  - Robot must carry enough battery for full work shift (8+ hours)
  - Large battery = heavy robot = slower = more power per meter
  - Battery is 30-40% of chassis weight and cost

  With parasitic charging (post every 50m):
  - Robot needs battery for 30-60 minutes of operation between charges
  - 5-10 minute top-off charges during natural task transitions
  - Small battery = lighter robot = faster = less power per meter
  - Battery is 15-20% of chassis weight and cost
  - More battery budget goes to toolhead power delivery

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Smaller batteries. Lighter robots. Faster movement. Lower cost. Longer
  fleet life. All because the charging infrastructure is everywhere.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

5. Power Routing Intelligence

House Brain manages the power network as a resource, not just a
collection of individual posts.

5.1 Inputs to Power Router

  - Solar generation per house (from house energy telemetry)
  - Current robot charge states (from chassis_health_record)
  - Post availability (from post status telemetry)
  - Construction schedule (which robots need to be where, when)
  - Weather forecast (cloud cover affects solar generation)
  - Time of day (solar generation curve)

5.2 Routing Decisions

  - Which post should this robot charge at? (nearest available with
    sufficient power)
  - Should this robot charge now or finish current task first? (SoC
    threshold vs task remaining time)
  - Should this house reduce residential load to prioritize
    construction charging? (only with homeowner consent via House Brain
    dashboard)
  - Should the swarm pause non-critical tasks during low-generation
    hours? (cloudy days, late afternoon)

5.3 Telemetry

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  charging_session_record

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  ---------------- ------------------------------------------------------
  Source           Charging Post (via house energy system)

  Emit Trigger     On charge session complete

  Required Fields  post_id, house_id, chassis_id, start_soc_pct,
                   end_soc_pct, charge_duration_s, avg_charge_rate_w,
                   solar_generation_w_during, grid_available_w,
                   timestamp

  Optional Fields  queue_wait_s, interrupted, interrupt_reason
  ---------------- ------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  charge_network_health_record

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  ---------------- ------------------------------------------------------
  Source           House Brain (aggregated)

  Emit Trigger     Every 60 seconds

  Required Fields  total_posts, posts_available, posts_charging,
                   posts_faulted, total_generation_w,
                   total_consumption_w, avg_robot_soc_pct,
                   robots_below_20_pct, robots_below_5_pct

  Optional Fields  estimated_hours_until_sunset, cloud_cover_pct
  ---------------- ------------------------------------------------------

6. Resilience

6.1 Single Post Failure

  - Post LED goes Red
  - House Brain removes from available pool
  - Robots rerouted to next nearest post
  - Repair advisory generated (Red/Green Protocol)

6.2 House Solar Failure

  - House energy system reports reduced generation
  - Post LED goes Amber (limited power) or Off (no power)
  - House Brain reduces charging rate or removes post
  - Adjacent house posts absorb demand

6.3 Weather Event (Dust Storm, Extended Cloud Cover)

  - All posts report reduced generation
  - House Brain enters power-conservation mode:
    - Non-critical robots park and sleep
    - Critical-path robots (active Assembly, active Sealer) prioritized
    - Battery swap from Factory-in-a-Box inventory deployed as backup
  - Swarm resumes normal operation when generation recovers

6.4 Night Operations

  - Solar generation drops to zero
  - House batteries provide reduced-rate charging
  - House Brain schedules non-time-critical tasks (Logistics lane
    maintenance, Verification idle weathering) for daytime
  - Night shift: only robots with sufficient charge continue critical
    tasks
  - Pre-dawn: robots park at posts for sunrise charging burst

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The system degrades gracefully. It never hard-fails from a power
  shortage — it slows down, prioritizes, and waits for energy.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

7. Integration with Existing Architecture

7.1 House Brain

  - Charging posts register as infrastructure nodes in House Brain
  - Power routing is a new scheduling dimension alongside construction
    task scheduling
  - Dashboard shows power network health alongside construction
    progress

7.2 Skateboard Chassis

  - Charging port is standard equipment on every chassis (already
    defined in Skateboard blueprint Section 3.3)
  - Battery BMS handles charge negotiation at the electrical level
  - Chassis compute handles logical handshake with post

7.3 Factory-in-a-Box

  - Container solar array provides the Day Zero charging backbone
  - Container battery inventory provides emergency swap capability
  - Container itself is the first node in the parasitic network

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The power network is not a separate system. It is embedded in the
  construction output. Every house is simultaneously shelter for a
  family and infrastructure for the swarm. The city powers itself into
  existence.
