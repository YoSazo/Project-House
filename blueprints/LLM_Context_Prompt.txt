===========================================================================
  PART 1 — WHAT PROJECT HOUSE IS
===========================================================================

Project House is building an autonomous construction system where robot
swarms build houses from raw earth, then scale exponentially — each
completed house powers the robots building the next ones.

Core architecture:
- A "House Brain" server (Node.js + PostgreSQL + React dashboard)
  orchestrates all robots through stigmergic mediation — robots never
  talk to each other directly. They write truth to a shared database.
  The House Brain reads that truth and dispatches work.
- One Universal Skateboard Chassis — a standardized 4-wheel-drive base
  carrying power (48V LiFePO4), compute, mobility, localization (UWB),
  and Red/Green LED diagnostics.
- 7 swappable toolheads snap onto the Skateboard via a single Universal
  Toolhead Interface (UTI): Survey, Excavator, Fabricator, Verification,
  Assembly, Sealer, Logistics.
- A Parasitic Power Network where every completed house includes a solar
  array and exterior walk-in charging dock, creating a distributed power
  grid that grows with construction.
- A Factory-in-a-Box (40-foot shipping container) that serves as the
  deployment seed: assembly bay, spare parts, initial charging docks,
  repair hub.
- A Red/Green Diagnostic Protocol: every replaceable module on every
  robot is either Green (good) or Red (needs service), verified by LED
  state, enabling field swap by non-experts.

The system is designed so that Batch 1 robots charge at docks built on
House 10,000. Same chassis. Same dock. Same voltage. Same protocol.
Infinite interchangeability.

===========================================================================
  PART 3 — WHAT CONSTRAINT TRANSMUTATION IS
===========================================================================

Constraint transmutation is the engineering problem-solving methodology
used throughout this project. It is NOT brainstorming, NOT elimination,
NOT compromise, and NOT the standard "trade study" method.

Here is exactly how it works:

A. You start with an engineering wall — a problem that looks impossible
   because it compresses too many contradictory demands into one point.

B. You do NOT try to solve the wall directly. You do NOT try to find a
   clever component that does everything at once. You do NOT compromise
   by weakening your requirements.

C. Instead, you TRADE the constraint. You identify what specific burden
   makes the wall impossible, and you move that burden — not eliminate
   it — into a form that is more manageable. The total difficulty is
   conserved. You are not getting something for nothing. You are
   choosing WHERE the difficulty lives.

D. Each trade creates a new, smaller wall. You repeat. Each step is
   called a "transmutation."

E. The chain continues until the remaining burden is operational and
   manageable — things like maintenance intervals, spare part inventory,
   inspection cadence, service procedures. Those are good burdens.
   "Hope the latch is magical" is a bad burden.

F. Progress is tracked as a percentage. Each transmutation is assigned
   a progress score reflecting how much of the original impossible
   demand has been traded into manageable form.

G. Transmutation chains can INHERIT from prior chains. If Chain A
   already solved part of the problem space, Chain B starts at a
   nonzero percentage ("inherited wins").

Key properties of transmutation:
- It is explicit. Every step names the old burden and the new burden.
- It is auditable. You can trace backward through the chain.
- It uses analogies to make each constraint viscerally clear before the
  trade (e.g., "backpack on a broomstick," "sword in a sheath,"
  "Chinese finger trap," "blind person threading a needle").
- The final form is never "perfect." It is always "the remaining burden
  is manageable and honestly stated."
- Each chain produces a doctrine — a set of design rules that encode
  the trades made so that future work respects them.

===========================================================================
  PART 4 — THE THREE TRANSMUTATION CHAINS AND WHAT THEY SOLVED
===========================================================================

The project started with conventional blueprint specifications that
described the system's intent but contained several engineering walls —
places where the spec hand-waved over real physical difficulty. An AI
review identified 6 concerns. Three of those required transmutation
chains to resolve.

---------------------------------------------------------------------------
  CHAIN 1: Toolhead Mount + Load Path Validation Stack (11 transmutations)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ORIGINAL SPEC (before transmutation):
  The skateboard chassis had a "cam-lock" quick-swap system where the
  toolhead snapped onto the chassis. All loads — transport AND active
  work — flowed through this mount into the chassis. The spec claimed
  "tool-free" swap and stated numbers like 120kg toolhead mass and
  1200W working power without engineering validation of how a quick-
  swap latch survives those loads under vibration, dirt, and thermal
  cycling.

  THE WALL:
  "How do you let a robot swap heavy working toolheads quickly, while
  ensuring the mount and load path remain field-worthy under real
  abuse?"

  HOW TRANSMUTATION SOLVED IT (in contrast to the original):

  The original spec treated the chassis mount as a structural hero —
  it had to do everything: carry the toolhead, absorb work reaction
  forces, survive impacts, release quickly. That is too many jobs for
  one interface.

  The transmutation chain traded that impossible demand through 11
  steps:

  1.  "The Skateboard is just the Uber" — Split transport from work.
      The toolhead self-grounds during operation (deploys anchor feet
      to soil). Active forces bypass the chassis entirely. The mount
      only sees dead-weight transport loads.

  2.  "iPhone case" — Change from deep insertion sleeve to side-
      clamping geometry, reducing jamming sensitivity.

  3.  "Wolff's Law grip" — Shape the mount so work loads drive the
      toolhead tighter into seating surfaces instead of loosening it.
      Stress seats the structure.

  4.  "External reset" — Move release from chaotic field conditions to
      a controlled unlock mechanism.

  5.  "Put the unlock on the body" — Distribute unlock capability to
      every chassis instead of bottlenecking at stations.

  6.  "Key appears only during contact" — Unlock mechanism is dormant
      until proper insertion begins.

  7.  "iPhone 12 case outline" — Keyed geometry so only the correct
      toolhead shape can initiate unlock. Random loads cannot.

  8.  "Kenya outlet" — Interface stays normally closed. Opens only when
      the correct keyed process actuates it. Dirt blocked.

  9.  "Pressure, not choreography" — Opening mechanism is force-
      threshold based, not precision-timed. Field-robust.

  10. "Coin slot recessed trigger" — Activation requires access path +
      recessed reach + internal pressure. Random impacts cannot trigger.

  11. "Give the slot a TTL" — Stop trying to make the wear surface
      eternal. Make it a sacrificial replaceable insert with a known
      service life.

  FINAL FORM (what the spec says NOW):
  "A self-grounding, self-seating, keyed, normally closed, pressure-
  opened, recessed-trigger, sacrificial-interface toolhead mount."

  The chassis is a taxi. The toolhead does the work. The mount is a
  service interface with bounded wear, not a structural fantasy.

  WHAT CHANGED IN THE BLUEPRINTS:
  - universal_skateboard_chassis.txt Section 4.1 completely rewritten
  - universal_skateboard_chassis.txt Section 7 (assembly procedure)
    updated
  - All 5 robot addendums updated with self-seating mount references
  - master_interface_contract.txt inventory updated

---------------------------------------------------------------------------
  CHAIN 2: Tool-Free Retention + Truth-of-Lock Stack (5 transmutations)
  (Inherits from Chain 1 — starts at 44%)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ORIGINAL SPEC (before transmutation):
  The spec said "tool-free" quick-swap with quarter-turn cam locks.
  It sounded fast and elegant but did not address how a tool-free
  lock remains reliable under vibration loosening, thermal cycling,
  dust contamination, and wear over thousands of cycles.

  THE WALL:
  "How do you make a tool-free quick-lock system remain reliable under
  real-world abuse without turning it into a fragile fantasy?"

  HOW THIS DIFFERS FROM CHAIN 1:
  Chain 1 solved the LOAD PATH — where forces go. Chain 2 solves the
  RETENTION TRUTH — how do you know it is actually locked, and how do
  you keep that answer trustworthy?

  Chain 2 inherits 44% progress from Chain 1 because the load path
  separation already de-loaded the lock. The lock no longer has to be
  the structural hero. But it still has to positively retain, resist
  vibration, verify its own state, and age gracefully.

  TRANSMUTATION CHAIN:

  1.  Auto-lock on insertion, deliberate vehicle release — Lock is a
      positive state, not friction. Vibration fights a locked geometry,
      not preload.

  2.  Two-step verification — Release requires two independent
      confirmation steps, rejecting electrical noise and accidental
      commands.

  3.  Physical alignment + pressure as truth — "Locked" means correct
      keyed geometry + correct depth + real force through intended
      route. Physical truth harder to fake than command truth.

  4.  Geometry-gated pressure + duration — Truth requires: keyed access
      geometry + recessed contact path + pressure threshold + sustained
      dwell time. Rejects accidental impacts, brief spikes, transient
      bumps.

  5.  TTL / replace the judge before it lies — The truth mechanism is a
      service module with known life. Inspected and replaced before
      unreliable. Wear exists in a bounded, serviceable layer.

  FINAL FORM:
  "A load-relieved, positively retained, physically verified, TTL-
  bounded tool-free lock system."

  WHAT CHANGED IN THE BLUEPRINTS:
  - universal_skateboard_chassis.txt Section 4.1 gained "Retention
    Verification (Truth-of-Lock)" subsection with 4-point physical
    verification criteria and mount_state UNCERTAIN reporting

---------------------------------------------------------------------------
  CHAIN 3: Autonomous Charging Dock + Final-Pose Truth Stack
  (7 transmutations)
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

  ORIGINAL SPEC (before transmutation):
  The parasitic power network spec described charging as: "Connector:
  standardized industrial DC connector, keyed, IP65" with "Cable reach:
  1.5m retractable coiled cable" and "Physical connection — robot docks
  charging port to post cable." This assumed a cable-based plug-in
  connection. Autonomous connector mating is a hard robotics problem.
  The spec hand-waved over it. If a human must plug the cable in, the
  autonomy story breaks.

  THE WALL:
  "How do you make robot charging autonomous when physical connector
  mating is a hard robotics problem?"

  HOW THIS DIFFERS FROM CHAINS 1 AND 2:
  Chains 1 and 2 dealt with the toolhead-to-chassis interface. Chain 3
  deals with a completely different interface: robot-to-infrastructure
  charging. But the METHOD is identical — trade the impossible demand
  step by step.

  The original spec's approach was to make the robot good at plugging
  things in. The transmutation approach was to eliminate plugging
  entirely.

  TRANSMUTATION CHAIN:

  1.  Turn plugging into walking in — Robot doesn't mate a connector.
      It drives into an outward-facing capture geometry. Simply walk in.

  2.  Make wrong entry spend itself into right entry — Self-correcting
      funnel geometry. The dock manufactures correctness from imperfect
      approach.

  3.  Spend time to save hardware — Low-battery mode: reduced speed,
      reduced acceleration, gentle approach. The dock corrects a gentle
      body, not a fast heavy one.

  4.  Let touch wake the final capture — First valid contact lets the
      dock finish the last bit of alignment. Robot only has to get
      close enough.

  5.  Add intent / session communication — Robot and dock communicate
      session intent before and during charging. Touch + handshake
      reduces ambiguity.

  6.  Charging physically impossible until final pose — Guide surfaces
      do correction. Charging contacts are recessed/isolated during
      approach. Only the one true final docked pose exposes the
      charging path. If charging = true, then full seating = true.

  7.  TTL / treat dock surfaces as service parts — Guide rails, capture
      surfaces, and contact pads are TTL-rated modules. The hard
      autonomy problem becomes routine maintenance.

  FINAL FORM:
  "A low-speed, self-correcting, walk-in charging dock whose geometry
  converts imperfect approach into one final seating pose, where
  electrical contact is physically impossible before full convergence,
  and whose guide/contact surfaces are TTL maintenance components."

  WHAT CHANGED IN THE BLUEPRINTS:
  - parasitic_power_network.txt Sections 2.2 and 2.3 completely
    rewritten (cable/connector language replaced with walk-in dock)
  - universal_skateboard_chassis.txt Section 3.3 updated (charging port
    → charging contacts for dock mating)
  - factory_in_a_box.txt updated (charging docks, spare contact pads
    instead of cables)
  - master_interface_contract.txt inventory updated to 13 documents

===========================================================================
  PART 5 — HOW TRANSMUTATION DIFFERS FROM CONVENTIONAL ENGINEERING
===========================================================================

A conventional engineering approach to these problems would have been:

  Original problem: "The latch might not hold 120kg under vibration."
  Conventional answer: "Use a stronger latch. Add bolts. Over-engineer
  the mount. Do FEA."

  Original problem: "Tool-free might not be reliable."
  Conventional answer: "Add more latches. Add redundancy. Tighten
  tolerances."

  Original problem: "Autonomous charging connector mating is hard."
  Conventional answer: "Use a robotic arm for plug insertion. Add
  camera guidance. Use magnetic alignment."

The transmutation approach is fundamentally different:

  It does not try to solve the problem at the same level of abstraction
  where it was stated. It asks: "What specific burden makes this
  impossible?" and trades that burden into a different form. It accepts
  that total difficulty is conserved — you cannot make hard problems
  free. But you CAN choose where the difficulty lives.

  - Instead of a stronger latch → make the latch not carry the load
  - Instead of more latches → make one lock whose truth is physically
    verified
  - Instead of a smarter plug → eliminate the plug entirely

  The convention strengthens solutions. Transmutation changes what the
  solution IS.

  The convention optimizes within constraints. Transmutation trades the
  constraints themselves.

  The result is not "a better version of the original." It is a
  fundamentally different architecture that inherits the original's
  goals but refuses to inherit its structural assumptions.

===========================================================================
  PART 6 — COMMON PATTERNS ACROSS ALL THREE CHAINS
===========================================================================

Several recurring moves appear across all three chains:

  1. LOAD SEPARATION — Don't make one interface do two jobs. Split
     transport from work. Split correction from contact. Split
     retention from structural truth.

  2. GEOMETRY AS TRUTH — Physical geometry replaces electronic signals
     as the primary proof of correct state. If the shape is right, the
     state is right. Geometry is harder to fake than data.

  3. SELF-CORRECTING SYSTEMS — Design accepts imperfect input and
     manufactures correct output. The mount self-seats under load. The
     dock self-corrects approach angle. Perfection is not required at
     entry.

  4. NORMALLY CLOSED / FAIL SAFE — Interfaces stay closed, locked, or
     de-energized when not actively engaged. Dirt cannot attack what is
     protected. Failure mode is NO-ACTION, not WRONG-ACTION.

  5. TTL OVER IMMORTALITY — Stop trying to make precision parts last
     forever. Treat them as service modules with known lifespans.
     Inspect, replace, continue. Every chain ends with a TTL
     transmutation as the final step.

  6. DELIBERATE ACTUATION — Nothing activates on accident. Locks require
     deliberate command. Docks require deliberate approach. Truth
     requires deliberate geometry + force + dwell time.

  7. ANALOGY-FIRST REASONING — Every constraint is made viscerally clear
     through a physical analogy before any trade is proposed. This
     prevents abstract hand-waving and forces the engineer to feel the
     problem before solving it.

===========================================================================
  PART 8 — THE VERTICAL EXPONENTIAL (HUMAN LOOP)
===========================================================================

The horizontal loop is mechanical: robots build house → house grows
power grid → power grid charges robots → robots build more houses.
That scales linearly.

The vertical loop is human: robots build house → family receives house →
family builds robots → more robots build more houses. That is the
multiplier that turns linear into exponential.

The deal: a family receives a completed house (CSEB walls, solar roof,
water harvesting, rooftop garden, biodigester, mesh internet) with zero
rent, zero mortgage, zero debt, zero utility bills. In return:

  Phase A: One-week training residency at the Factory-in-a-Box
  Phase B: Assembly of one complete robot cluster (7 Skateboards +
           7 toolheads) — embedded within the training week
  Phase C: 4 hours/week of field maintenance for 6 months

  Total obligation: ~200 hours. Then the house is theirs forever.

The math: a Skateboard takes 15 minutes to assemble (no tools). A
toolhead mounts in 60 seconds. A full cluster is ~2 hours of hands-on
assembly. The training week wraps learning around that assembly.

Growth: Week 0 has 1 cluster. Week 1 produces House 1 + Family 1
builds Cluster B. Week 2 has 2 clusters, produces 2 houses. Each
house adds a charging node (horizontal) AND a trained family with a
new cluster (vertical). Growth accelerates with every house.

Skill progression is emergent:
  Level 0: Any resident reads Red/Green LEDs
  Level 1: Trained household assembles Skateboards, does field swaps
  Level 2: Experienced maintainer diagnoses patterns, trains new
           families
  Level 3: Repair Hub technician does component-level repair
  Level 4: Local engineer designs/fabricates components

No one is hired or assigned a level. The system creates conditions
for progression. The training week is Level 1. Everything after is
emergent.

The house is never repossessed. The obligation is social, not legal.
Failure modes have recovery paths. The goal is post-scarcity, not
post-scarcity with an asterisk.

===========================================================================
  PART 9 — DATABASE SCHEMA (KEY TABLES)
===========================================================================

The schema reflects the chassis/toolhead separation:

  robots table:
  - id, cluster_id, name, status
  - chassis_id (physical chassis serial — Skateboard identity)
  - toolhead_id (currently mounted toolhead serial, NULL = bare chassis)
  - toolhead_type (current toolhead type for quick lookup)
  - pos_x, pos_y, pos_z, busy_until, active_house_id
  - work statistics (total_work_seconds, placements, retries, failures)

  toolhead_pairings table (swap history):
  - chassis_id, toolhead_id, toolhead_type
  - paired_at, unpaired_at, paired_by (event that triggered the swap)

This lets the system track which chassis carried which toolhead, when
swaps happened, and who/what triggered them.

===========================================================================
  PART 10 — HOW TO WORK WITH THIS CONTEXT
===========================================================================

When working on this project:

  1. Respect the transmutation chains. They are not suggestions — they
     are load-bearing engineering decisions. If you change the mount
     architecture, the charging dock, or the retention system, trace
     your change through the relevant chain to confirm it doesn't
     reintroduce a burden that was already traded away.

  2. The remaining burdens are documented. Each chain ends with a
     "Remaining Living Constraint" section listing what is NOT solved —
     that is the honest frontier. Work there, not behind it.

  3. Analogies are load-bearing. They are not decoration. If you cannot
     explain a new constraint through a physical analogy, you may not
     fully understand it yet.

  4. TTL is doctrine, not laziness. Every precision surface in the
     system is a service part. This is a deliberate engineering choice,
     not a failure to build something permanent.

  5. The House Brain is the only coordinator. Robots read and write to
     the database. They do not negotiate with each other. If you add a
     new robot behavior, it must go through the stigmergic mediation
     pattern: robot writes truth → House Brain reads → House Brain
     dispatches.

  6. "Solved" means "remaining burden is manageable and named." It does
     not mean "no difficulty remains." If someone says a problem is
     solved, look for the remaining living constraint to understand what
     honest burden was accepted.
