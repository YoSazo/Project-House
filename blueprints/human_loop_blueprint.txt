  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Core Doctrine: A family does not pay for their house. They pay it
  forward by building the robots that build the next family's house.
  The vertical loop — house produces family produces robots produces
  house — is the mechanism that makes growth exponential rather than
  linear. The assembly is engineered to be completable by anyone.
  The obligation is engineered to be lighter than any mortgage.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  One-Sentence Summary: Every family that receives a house assembles one
  complete robot cluster (7 Skateboards + 7 toolheads) during a one-week
  training residency at the Factory-in-a-Box, then contributes 4 hours
  per week of maintenance labor for their first 6 months as residents —
  after which the obligation is complete and the house is theirs outright,
  no rent, no mortgage, no debt.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Purpose

The horizontal loop is mechanical: robots build house, house grows power
grid, power grid charges robots, robots build more houses. That loop
scales linearly — one cluster produces ~1.5 houses per week regardless
of how many houses exist.

The vertical loop is human: robots build house → family receives house →
family builds robots → more robots build more houses. This is the
multiplier that turns linear into exponential.

Without the vertical loop, growth is bounded by the number of clusters
that ship inside the original container. With it, every house produces
the labor that creates the next cluster. The system manufactures its
own workforce.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The constraint was never materials, energy, or technology.
  The constraint was coordination.
  The Human Loop is the coordination solution for labor.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

2. The Deal

A family receives:
  - A completed house (CSEB walls, solar roof, water harvesting,
    rooftop garden, biodigester, mesh internet connection)
  - Zero rent. Zero mortgage. Zero debt. Zero bills for electricity,
    water, or cooking fuel
  - Permanent ownership after completing the obligation

A family contributes:
  - Phase A: One-week training residency at Factory-in-a-Box
  - Phase B: Assembly of one complete robot cluster (7 Skateboards +
    7 toolheads)
  - Phase C: 4 hours per week of maintenance labor for 6 months
  - Total obligation: ~1 week intensive + ~104 hours spread over
    6 months

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Compare this to a 30-year mortgage at $800/month = $288,000.
  Compare this to a lifetime of rent that builds zero equity.
  The family's total obligation is approximately 200 hours of labor.
  After that, the house is theirs forever. No payments. No landlord.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

3. Why One Cluster

  The number is not arbitrary. Here is the math:

  One robot cluster = 7 Skateboards + 7 toolheads.

  Skateboard assembly time: ~15 minutes per chassis (snap-fit wheels,
  slide-in battery, seat compute module, seat motor driver, connect
  sensor harness — no tools required).

  Toolhead mounting time: ~60 seconds per toolhead (keyed profile seats
  it, single UTI connector).

  Total hands-on assembly for one cluster: ~2 hours.

  But assembly alone is not the obligation. The family also learns the
  system. The one-week residency exists because:

  - Assembly is only valuable if the builder understands what they are
    building
  - Red/Green maintenance requires pattern recognition that comes from
    handling the modules during assembly
  - The training week produces not just one cluster but one trained
    household that can perform field maintenance on any robot in the
    city for decades
  - The city's maintenance capacity scales with its population

  One cluster is the right number because:

  - One cluster produces ~1.5 houses per week. The family's cluster
    will build houses for other families, paying the gift forward
    indefinitely
  - The assembly is completable within the training week with time to
    spare — no family is stuck in an impossible obligation
  - The parts for one cluster are already budgeted in the Factory-in-
    a-Box seed inventory (8 frames, 40 wheels, 12 batteries, etc.)
  - Each new cluster adds net-new construction capacity to the city,
    so the city accelerates with every family

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  A 30-year mortgage binds a family to a bank.
  A 200-hour obligation binds a family to their neighbors.
  When the obligation ends, the family owns a home and a city that
  builds itself around them.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

4. Phase A — Training Residency (1 Week)

The training week happens at the Factory-in-a-Box. It follows the same
principle as the robot system: structured progression, visible feedback,
no abstraction before hands-on experience.

  Day 1: Orientation + Disassembly

  - Tour of Factory-in-a-Box (all four zones)
  - Introduction to the Red/Green diagnostic protocol — what the LEDs
    mean, what Green looks like, what Red looks like
  - Supervised disassembly of a training Skateboard (not a production
    unit): remove wheels, slide out battery, unseat compute module,
    disconnect harness
  - Purpose: the family handles every component before they assemble
    anything. Disassembly builds familiarity without stakes

  Day 2: Skateboard Assembly (First 3 Chassis)

  - Family assembles their first 3 Skateboards from kit using the
    assembly jigs in Zone A
  - Each chassis: lay frame → snap wheels → slide battery → seat
    compute → seat motor driver → connect harness → power on →
    self-test
  - Supervised by a trained resident (Phase 2+ family or site lead)
  - Each chassis takes ~15 minutes. Three chassis = ~1 hour of assembly
    + ~2 hours of explanation, questions, mistakes, redo
  - All three chassis must pass self-test (LEDs go through startup
    sequence, report to House Brain) before proceeding

  Day 3: Skateboard Assembly (Remaining 4 Chassis)

  - Family assembles chassis 4–7 with decreasing supervision
  - By chassis 5–7, the family should be assembling independently with
    the supervisor observing only
  - All 7 chassis online and reporting to House Brain by end of day

  Day 4: Toolhead Mounting + System Test

  - Family mounts all 7 toolheads onto their 7 chassis
  - Each toolhead: position over mount → lower into self-seating taper
    → confirm retention → connect UTI connector → handshake confirmed
  - Toolhead mounting is fast (~60 seconds each) but the family learns
    to read the handshake confirmation on the dashboard
  - Full cluster registered with House Brain: 7 robots, 7 toolheads,
    all telemetry flowing
  - Afternoon: cluster runs a supervised test operation (short Survey
    scan of a test patch, one Excavator cycle, one Fabricator press) —
    the family watches their robots work for the first time

  Day 5: Red/Green Maintenance Practicum

  - Introduction to field maintenance: identify Red module → swap with
    Green spare from Zone B → return Red to Repair Hub in Zone D
  - Supervised practice: deliberate Red insertions on the training
    Skateboard (swap a wheel module, swap a battery, swap a compute
    board)
  - Practice reading the dashboard: which robot is reporting Red,
    which module, what is the recommended action
  - Introduction to the maintenance shift schedule (Phase C)

  Day 6: Repair Hub Exposure (Optional Track)

  - For family members who show interest: introduction to Zone D
    (Repair Hub)
  - Observe diagnostic procedure: Red module intake, multimeter test,
    visual inspection, soldering station demo
  - This day is NOT required. It exists because every city needs
    residents who self-select into deeper technical roles. This is
    where that selection happens naturally
  - Families that skip Day 6 spend it on additional field maintenance
    practice instead

  Day 7: Handoff + House Tour

  - Family's cluster is formally registered as a production unit
  - Cluster assigned its first house build by the House Brain scheduler
  - Family tours their own house (if already complete) or watches
    construction progress on their assigned lot
  - Maintenance shift schedule confirmed
  - Training residency complete

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  After 7 days, the family has:
  - Built 7 robots with their own hands
  - Watched those robots perform real work
  - Learned to read Red/Green diagnostics
  - Practiced field module swaps
  - Earned a house

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

5. Phase B — Cluster Assembly (During Training Week)

Phase B is embedded within Phase A. The cluster assembly IS the training
curriculum. There is no separate "classroom" phase and "assembly" phase.
The family learns by building.

  Assembly Bill of Materials (per cluster):

  Skateboard components:
  - 7 × frame plate
  - 28 × wheel module (4 per chassis)
  - 7 × battery pack (LiFePO4 48V)
  - 7 × compute module (ESP32-S3 or Pi CM4)
  - 7 × motor driver board
  - 7 × sensor harness

  Toolheads:
  - 1 × Survey toolhead
  - 1 × Excavator toolhead
  - 1 × Fabricator toolhead
  - 1 × Verification toolhead
  - 1 × Assembly toolhead
  - 1 × Sealer toolhead
  - 1 × Logistics toolhead

  Total assembly time (hands-on): ~2 hours
  Total training week time (including learning): ~40 hours
  Tools required: zero (all snap-fit, slide-in, keyed connector)

  Quality gate: every Skateboard must pass power-on self-test. Every
  toolhead must complete UTI handshake. Cluster must appear in House
  Brain dashboard with all 7 robots reporting Green telemetry. If any
  unit fails self-test, the family debugs it with supervisor guidance
  (this is the most valuable learning in the whole week).

6. Phase C — Maintenance Commitment (6 Months)

After moving into their house, the family contributes 4 hours per week
of maintenance labor to the city's robot fleet. This is not indentured
servitude — it is neighborhood infrastructure maintenance, no different
from mowing a shared lawn or participating in a barn raising.

  What maintenance labor looks like:

  - Field patrol: walk the active construction zone, visually check
    robots for Red LEDs, report anomalies via the dashboard app
  - Module swap: when a robot reports Red, retrieve a Green spare from
    the nearest Factory-in-a-Box or satellite parts cache, swap the
    module, return the Red module to the Repair Hub
  - Charging dock inspection: visual check of walk-in dock guide
    surfaces and contact pads, flag any that look worn per TTL schedule
  - Battery rotation: swap depleted battery packs on robots in the
    field with charged packs from inventory (slide-in swap, 30 seconds)
  - Inventory check: count spares in Zone B bins, confirm counts match
    House Brain inventory system, flag discrepancies

  Shift schedule:

  - 4 hours per week, scheduled by House Brain into 2 × 2-hour shifts
    or 4 × 1-hour shifts depending on family preference and city needs
  - Shifts assigned by zone and robot cluster — the family primarily
    maintains robots in their own neighborhood
  - House Brain tracks shift completion via check-in/check-out on the
    dashboard app
  - Shift swaps allowed between families (coordinated on dashboard)

  Duration: 6 months from house move-in date.

  After 6 months:
  - The obligation is COMPLETE
  - The house is owned outright — no further payments, labor, or
    conditions of any kind
  - The family may voluntarily continue maintenance shifts (many will —
    it becomes routine and social)
  - Families that continue earning maintenance hours after obligation
    ends accumulate credit toward: priority on additional floors
    (governance streak), preferred lot selection for family members,
    apprenticeship priority at the Repair Hub

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The 6-month maintenance phase exists for two reasons:
  1. The city needs distributed maintenance capacity as the fleet grows
  2. The family remains connected to the system that built their home
     long enough for maintenance to become habit rather than obligation

  After 6 months, the family knows their neighborhood robots by sight.
  They notice a wobbling wheel before the telemetry does. They are the
  city's immune system.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

7. The Exponential Math

Here is why this closes the loop.

  Assumptions:
  - 1 cluster produces ~1.5 houses per week
  - 1 house produces 1 family
  - 1 family produces 1 cluster (during training week)
  - Training week overlaps with house construction (family trains
    while their house is being built by an existing cluster)

  Week 0:  1 cluster (seed). 0 houses. 0 families.
  Week 1:  Cluster A builds House 1 (+ starts House 2).
           Family 1 enters training, builds Cluster B.
  Week 2:  2 clusters operating. Cluster A finishes House 2.
           Family 2 enters training, builds Cluster C.
  Week 3:  3 clusters operating. 3 houses/week production rate.
           Family 3 enters training, builds Cluster D.
  Week 4:  4 clusters. ~6 houses/week.
  Week 8:  8 clusters. ~12 houses/week. 8 trained families.
  Week 16: ~16 clusters. ~24 houses/week. Parasitic power grid is
           dense. Maintenance labor pool is 16+ families.
  Week 26: ~26 clusters. ~39 houses/week. First families completing
           their 6-month obligation — now contributing voluntarily.

  The growth is not perfectly exponential because:
  - Component supply chain has lead time (parts must be ordered/shipped)
  - Training throughput is limited by Factory-in-a-Box space (one family
    training at a time per container, unless multiple containers)
  - Not every family's cluster deploys immediately (may wait for
    component delivery)

  But it is FASTER than linear because every house completion adds both
  a charging node (horizontal) AND a trained family with a new cluster
  (vertical). The growth rate accelerates even if each step is not an
  instant doubling.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The constraint on growth is not labor. The vertical loop solved labor.
  The constraint on growth is not energy. The parasitic network solved
  energy.
  The constraint on growth is component supply — how fast parts arrive
  from Shenzhen (Phase 1-3) or local manufacturing (Phase 4).
  That is a logistics problem, not an engineering problem. Logistics
  problems have known solutions.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

8. Component Supply Model

The vertical loop creates demand for robot components. That demand must
be met or the loop stalls.

  Phase 1 (Weeks 0–12): Container Seed Inventory

  The Factory-in-a-Box ships with enough components for ~3 clusters
  (seed cluster + 2 family-built clusters). This is already specified
  in the container inventory:
  - 8 frames (7 active + 1) — enough for 1 extra cluster with
    reorder
  - 40 wheels (32 active + 8) — enough for 2 extra clusters
  - 12 batteries (7 active + 5) — enough for 1+ extra cluster
  - 10 compute modules, 10 motor drivers, 10 sensor harnesses —
    enough for ~1.5 extra clusters each

  Reorder triggers automatically when inventory drops below threshold.
  House Brain generates procurement advisory with exact quantities.

  Phase 2 (Weeks 12–52): Bulk Procurement Pipeline

  By week 12, the site has produced enough houses to demonstrate
  viability. Component orders shift from seed inventory to regular
  bulk procurement:
  - Skateboard frame plates: steel/aluminum fabrication, ordered in
    batches of 50–100
  - Wheel modules: standardized industrial motors + tires, bulk pricing
  - Battery packs: LiFePO4 cells, assembled into standard packs at
    origin or on-site
  - Compute modules: off-the-shelf ESP32/Pi CM4, bulk from distributor
  - Motor drivers, sensor harnesses: PCB assembly from Shenzhen
  - Toolheads: assembled from sub-components at Factory-in-a-Box
    Zone A or at regional assembly hub

  Funding: component procurement funded by carbon credit revenue from
  solar displacement + knowledge export income + tourism revenue +
  city fund. See EmprOS economic model.

  Phase 3 (Year 2+): Local Manufacturing Transition

  As specified in Factory-in-a-Box Phase 4:
  - Simple components manufactured locally (frames, brackets, harnesses)
  - Complex components still sourced externally but at volume pricing
  - Toolhead designs published as open protocol — local and global
    community designs new capabilities
  - Supply chain dependency decreases as local capacity increases

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The economic engine: houses produce solar power → solar displacement
  earns carbon credits → carbon credits fund component procurement →
  components become robots → robots build houses. The money loop
  mirrors the physical loop.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

9. Skill Progression Path

The training week is the entry point. But the city needs people at
every skill level. The Human Loop creates a natural progression:

  Level 0 — Any Resident (no training required)
  - Read Red/Green LEDs on any robot or module
  - Report anomalies via dashboard app
  - Basic awareness: "that robot has a red light, someone should look"

  Level 1 — Trained Household (after training week)
  - Assemble Skateboards from kit (15 min per chassis)
  - Mount toolheads (60 sec per toolhead)
  - Perform field module swaps (Red → Green replacement)
  - Swap batteries in the field (30 sec)
  - Read dashboard telemetry for their maintenance zone
  - Inspect charging dock surfaces for wear

  Level 2 — Experienced Maintainer (after ~3 months of shifts)
  - Diagnose common failure modes by pattern (e.g., "motor current
    spike + vibration anomaly = wheel bearing, not motor")
  - Perform multi-module diagnosis on a struggling robot
  - Train new families during their training week (peer training)
  - Manage inventory counts and reorder flags

  Level 3 — Repair Hub Technician (self-selected, after ~6 months)
  - Diagnose and repair Red modules at component level
  - Use multimeter, oscilloscope, soldering station
  - Flash firmware on compute modules
  - Perform PCB-level inspection and repair
  - Document failure modes in House Brain repair log
  - This role emerges naturally from curious residents — it is not
    assigned

  Level 4 — Local Engineer (Phase 4, after ~12+ months)
  - Design and fabricate simple components locally
  - Modify toolhead designs for local conditions
  - Contribute to open-protocol toolhead ecosystem
  - Train other sites via knowledge export
  - This role is the city teaching itself to build itself

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  No one is hired. No one is assigned a level. The system creates the
  conditions for progression and people self-select. The training week
  is Level 1. Everything after that is emergent.

10. Failure Modes and Safety Nets

What happens when the loop breaks?

  Family cannot complete training week:
  - Training extends by up to 3 additional days at no penalty
  - If a family member has a physical limitation, other family members
    or neighbors can fulfill the assembly role
  - Assembly requires no heavy lifting except Fabricator toolhead
    (~120kg, two-person lift) — all other components are under 25kg
  - If a family genuinely cannot complete assembly, they can fulfill
    the obligation with equivalent maintenance hours instead (40 hours
    of maintenance shifts in lieu of cluster assembly)

  Family does not complete maintenance shifts:
  - House Brain tracks shift completion
  - Missed shifts generate a gentle reminder, not a punishment
  - Consistently missed shifts after 3 months trigger a conversation
    with the neighborhood assembly (governance layer), not eviction
  - The house is NEVER repossessed. The obligation is social, not
    contractual-legal. The system runs on community trust, not legal
    enforcement
  - In practice, once families see their neighbors maintaining the
    robots that are building the neighborhood, social incentive is
    stronger than any contract

  Component supply stalls:
  - Cluster assembly pauses until parts arrive — family is not
    penalized for supply chain delays they don't control
  - Family moves into their house on schedule regardless
  - Assembly obligation resumes when parts are available
  - House Brain tracks incomplete clusters and automatically requeues
    assembly when inventory is restocked

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  The system is designed for real humans with real lives. Obligations
  are completable. Failure modes have recovery paths. The house is
  never at risk. The goal is post-scarcity, not post-scarcity with
  an asterisk.

11. Connection to Post-Scarcity Baseline

The Human Loop is what makes the following sentence true:

  "The marginal cost of adding another household to the system
  approaches zero because the material is the ground, the energy is
  the sun, the water is the rain, the labor is the community, and the
  robots replicate themselves."

  Material: soil excavated on-site → CSEB blocks. Cost: ~$350-650 in
  lime, lintels, glass, hardware. NOT zero, but approaching negligible
  at scale.

  Energy: solar panels on every roof. Parasitic power network grows
  with every house.

  Water: rainwater harvesting + filtration. No utility dependency.

  Food: rooftop garden + greywater irrigation + biodigester. Closed
  loop.

  Labor: the Human Loop. Every family trained. Every family contributes.
  No hired workforce. No NGO dependency. No external labor market.

  Robot replication: families assemble robots from kit. Robots build
  houses. Houses produce families. Families assemble robots. The circle
  is closed.

  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  This is not charity. Nothing is given.
  This is not a market. Nothing is sold.
  This is a system where the output of each step is the input of the
  next step, and the human is part of the loop — not as a consumer of
  a product, but as a builder of the system that builds their world.
